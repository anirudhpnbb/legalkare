Before returning any finding on the present revision petition, it is worthwhile to note the brief landscape of the case. Revisionist herein had filed a complaint under section 200 Cr.P.C. against the respondents for commission of the offence punishable under section 384 / 499 / 500 / 506 / 120-B / 34 IPC. Along with an application under section 156(3) of Cr.P.C. seeking investigation against the respondents pursuant to the registration of the FIR under the said sections. 5. The then Ld. CMM vide order dated 02.02.2023 declined to order registration of FIR and investigation by police under section 156(3) Cr.P.C. , however, cognizance of the complaint was taken under section 200 Cr.P.C. The said order was challenged by the revisonist before the Appellate Court and vide ordr dated 25.05.2023, the matter was remanded back to the court of Ld. CMM for fresh consideration on the application under section 156(3) Cr.P.C. Page No. 8 of 13 6. Vide order dated 31.05.2023, the then Ld. CMM directed to assign the matter to the court of Ld. ACMM, South District, who proceeded to hear the arguments afresh and vide detailed order dated 05.09.2023 dismissed the application under section 156(3) Cr.P.C. The main extract of the finding of Ld. ACMM merits reproduction as under: "The moot question in the instant matter is whether on the basis of the application filed by the applicant, a prima facie case of commission of cognizable offence is made out against the transcript of the conversation between proposed accused persons. In case, the answer is in affirmative, whether the case warrants investigation by police officials thereby warranting registration of FIR is the consequential question. The following points are pertinent to be noted: (a) The applicant has alleged offences under Sections 384 /499/500/506/ 304- B/34 of IPC . (b) Section 34 of IPC is not a substantive offence. (c) The offence of defamation ( Section 499 read with Section 500 ) and offence of criminal intimidation ( Section 506 ) are non-cognizable in nature. (d) Section 384 pertains to extortion which is a cognizable offence. The question is whether prima facie, an offence of extortion is made out from the instant application. The only averments qua extortion in the application is that the applicant has filed FIR No. 511/2021 with an intent to extort money from him. However, any material indicating towards such extortion ought to have been in the custody of the applicant and should have been placed on record. Furthermore, the said allegation actually pertains to a defense to the prosecution case in FIR No. 511/2021 which is sub- Page No. 9 of 13 judice. Prima facie, the application filed under Section 156(3) of the Code does not satisfy the essential ingredients to attract the alleged offence of extortion. As the allegations qua extortion are vague and are not specific, an order for registration of an FIR and investigation on the accusation of the commission of the offences alleged is not made out. Hence, the application under Section 156(3) of the Code is dismissed." 7. In so far as the allegations of defamation is concerned, as it is already noted above that offence of defamation is non cognizable and private in nature and revisionist is at liberty to pursue his remedies as per law. In so far as the offence of extortion is concerned, in the entire complaint, I do not find any allegation which would call for an investigation through the agency of the police. The lodging of the prosecution to evade the payment of balance professional fees of Rs. 15,000/- would not tantamount to the offence of extortion. Thus, on this count also, the order of Ld. ACMM cannot be faulted with and does not warrant any interference. 10. The revisionist, during the course of arguments, also harped upon the offence of criminal conspiracy lodged by him under section 120-B IPC against the respondent on the bedrock of some audio recordings wherein respondent no. 3 is allegedly speaking to some anti- social element to exterminate the revisionist. The said audio recordings contained in the pendrive annexed with the revision petition were played Page No. 11 of 13 in the open court, but there is no such utterance at the end of the speakers therein which could indicate that any criminal conspiracy was being hatched to kill the revisionist. Though, some utterances could be heard at the end of one female speaker, who is stated to be respondent no.3, that she is wishing for somebody to die which the revisionist has claimed was referring to him, but this circumstance in itself does not show in any manner that said female speaker is conspiring with the other person to kill the revisionist . Therefore, the offence of criminal conspiracy u/s 120B IPC also as alleged by revisionist is not made out. 11.
