Singh was the counsel on their behalf and he can not be prosecuted as such. 11 In Santokh Singh vs Izhar Hussain & another , AIR 1973 SC 2190, the Hon'ble Supreme Court has held that every incorrect or false statement does not make it incumbent on the Court to order prosecution. The Court has to exercise judicial discretion in the light of all the relevant circumstances when it determines the question of expediency. The Court orders prosecution in the larger interest of administration of justice and not to gratify feelings of personal revenge or vindictiveness or to serve the ends of a private party. Too frequent prosecutions for such offences tend to defeat its very object. It is only in glaring cases of deliberate falsehood where CR No.69/10 & 71/10 Raj Pal vs Jagdish etc. Page 7 of 10 conviction is highly likely, that the Court should direct prosecution. In the present case, contention of petitioner Rajpal that Savitri, Dharamvir, subhash, Devki and Tejender Dutta are liable to be prosecuted is negated in view of the fact that they were not the complainant rather they were just witnesses who deposed as per their wish on being summoned by the Court, so there is no basis to launch prosecution against them under Section 211 IPC. My this view is fortified by the law laid down in Santokh Singh 's ( supra) case. 12 Even otherwise, Ld. ACMM could not have taken cognizance against Jagdish as the complaint filed by Rajpal was barred under section 195(1)(b)(i) of Cr.P.C. Provisions of section 195(1)(b)(i) of Cr.P.C provide that no Court shall take cognizance of any offence punishable under Section 211 IPC when such offence is alleged to have been committed in, or in relation to, any proceedings in Court except on the complaint in writing of that Court. In the present case, the complaint was filed under Section 211 IPC by Rajpal being private party with regard to alleged offence of giving false evidence and instituting a false case, during the proceedings of Court. The complaint, if any, under Section 211 IPC, could have been made by the Court concerned in which the proceedings were conducted. In CR No.69/10 & 71/10 Raj Pal vs Jagdish etc. Page 8 of 10 similar circumstances, Hon'ble Bombay High Court in case Subhash Ramchandra Durge vs Deepak Annasaheb Gat , 2001(1) RCR (Criminal) 368, has held that Ld. Incharge Judicial Magistrate, First Class, committed obvious error in taking cognizance of the offence punishable under Section 211 IPC, ignoring bar of Section 195 Cr.P.C. As per provisions of Section 195(1)(b)(i) of Cr.P.C., if the complaint is filed under section 211 of IPC, the complaint has to be in writing and it has to be filed by none else but by the relevant Court. It means that if the complaint under section 211 of IPC is to be filed, it can not be filed by a private complainant. While observing this proposition of law, the Hon'ble Court quashed the order taking cognizance by the Magistrate. 13 In view of aforementioned facts and circumstances of the case and law of land discussed above, this Court is of the considered opinion that complaint filed by Rajpal against Jagdish and others is not sustainable as hit by section 195(1)(b)(i) of Cr.P.C. Consequently, impugned order dated 30.5.2010 is hereby set aside. As a result, C.R. No.69/2010 is dismissed and C.R. No.71/2010 is hereby allowed.
