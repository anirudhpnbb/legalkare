INGREDIENTS OF OFFENCE - 12. Before dwelling into the facts of the present case, it would be apposite to discuss the legal standards required to be met by both sides. In order to establish the offence under Section 500 IPC, the complainant/prosecution must fulfil all the essential ingredients of the offence. The offence of defamation is defined under Section 499 of the IPC. The essential ingredients of the offence, as explained by the Hon'ble Apex Court in Mohd. Abdulla Khan vs. Prakash K. (2018) 1 SCC 615, are as under-: "10. An analysis of the above reveals that to constitute an offence of defamation it requires a person to make some imputation concerning any other person; (1) Such imputation must be made either (a) With intention, or (b) Knowledge, or (c) Having reason to believe that such an imputation will harm the reputation of the person against whom the imputation is made. (II) Imputation could be, by (a) Words, either spoken or written, or (b) By making signs, or (c) Visible representations (II) Imputations could be either made or published. Ct. No. 4999490/2016 SUNIL PREM MASIH VS. H.N. POTTER Page 8 of 18 Digitally signed by DEV DEV CHAUDHARY CHAUDHARY Date: 2021.08.21 14:15:34 +05'30' The difference between making of an imputation and publishing the same is" If 'X' tells 'Y' that 'Y' is a criminal - 'X' makes an imputation If 'X' tells 'Z' that 'Y' is a criminal - 'X' publishes the imputation. The essence of publication in the context of Section 499 is the communication of defamatory imputation to the persons other than the persons against whom the imputation is made." The same was reiterated in Google India Private Limited vs. Visaka Industries (2020) 4 SCC 162. The provision also contains ten exceptions enumerating the circumstances in which the imputation will not be an offence. Pertinently, under Section 199 of the CrPC, a Court can take cognizance of the offence of defamation only on the complaint of the aggrieved person. 13. Needless to mention, in criminal law, the burden of proof on the prosecution is that of beyond reasonable doubt. The presumption of innocence of the accused has to be rebutted by the prosecution by adducing cogent evidence that points towards the guilt of the accused. The evidence in the present case is to be weighed keeping in view the above legal standards. PROOF OF DEFAMATORY LETTER - 14. The copy of documents appearing on record bears the stamp, "Dr. Kuljeet Jagridar, SMO (SG) Radiologist, MCI- 17579, DMC-70, Central Reserve Police Force". CW1/G does not fall within the definition of "public document" and could not have been proved even by obtaining certified copies. Under Section 74 of the Evidence Act, public documents are the documents that form the act or record of acts of sovereign authorities, official bodies and tribunals and public offices. Further, under Section 74(2) of the Evidence Act, public record of private documents kept by the State are also public documents. However, a letter written to a Government official does not ipso facto become a public document under the said provision. In other words, merely because a document is kept on a file of a government authority, it does not lose its character of being a private document. 18. In this regard, it has been held by the Hon'ble Gujarat High Court in State of Gujarat vs. Ambalal Maganlal Shah 1966 CrLJ 967, as under :- "1. It is contended that the compromise became a part of the record of the Court and that therefore it is a public document. Before the compromise was presented in this Court it was an act of the parties. It was, therefore, a private document. A private document does not become a public document simply because it is filed in the Court. To be a public document, it should be a record of the act of a public officer or Court. There is a distinction between the record of the act of the Court and the record of the Court. A document which forms part of the record of the Court does not necessarily form record of the act of the Court. It may be that upon a private document, which is a record of the act of private parties a second act is done by the public officer or by the Court, namely filing the document or putting a number on the document. Only that portion of the document, which records the act of the Court in filing the document would be a public document. Therefore, that part of the document, namely the original part would be a private document forming the record of the act of the private parties and what is subsequently added to that document by the Court would be a public document." The second essential ingredient is publication of the defamatory imputations. To prove publication, the complainant has relied upon the explanation notice, which is dated 10.12.2011 and is part of documents comprised in Ex. CW1/G. Vide the said letter, the comments of the complainant have been sought. Ld. counsel for the complainant has contended that since his comments were called due to receipt of the impugned letter by the Department, the publication of the impugned letter to the Department officials stands proved. 22. However, in absence of proof of the impugned letter itself, this document does not prove publication in any way. Pertinently, the addressee of the impugned letter, has not been called to the witness box by the complainant. The explanation letter was objected to at the time of marking as the complainant is not the author of the document. Although this document is a public document, the document does not reproduce the defamatory contents of the impugned letter and only refers to a copy of an undated letter sent by the accused. There is no specific mention of the impugned letter. Therefore, the fact remains that the identity of the letter mentioned in the document is not proved to be that of the impugned letter. In a criminal trial, assumptions cannot take place of proof. Therefore, the publication of the alleged defamatory contents also remains unproved. 23. Moving forward, the intent or knowledge to harm the reputation of a person is another essential ingredient of the offence under Section 499 IPC. The explanation appended to the said section, Digitally signed by Ct. No. 4999490/2016 SUNIL PREM MASIH VS. H.N. POTTER Page 14 of 18 DEV DEV CHAUDHARY CHAUDHARY Date: 2021.08.21 14:16:39 +05'30' is relevant for the present purposes and is reproduced hereinbelow - "Explanation 4- No imputation is said to harm a person's reputation, unless that imputation directly or indirectly, in the estimation of others, lowes the moral or intellectual character of that person, or lowers the character of that person in respect of his caste or his calling, or lowers the credit of that person, or causes it to be believed that the body of that person in in a loathsome state, or in a state generally considered as disgraceful." Further, the Hon'ble Apex Court in Subramanian Swamy vs. Union of India (2016) 7 SCC 221 has observed as under -: "168. For the aforesaid purpose, it is imperative to analyse in detail what constitutes the offence of "defamation" as provided under Section 499 IPC. To constitute the offence, there has to be imputation and it must have been made in the manner as provided in the provision with the intention of causing harm or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm the reputation of the person about whom it is made. Causing harm to the reputation of a person is the basis on which the offence is founded and mens rea is a condition precedent to constitute the said offence. The complainant has to show that the accused had intended or known or had reason to believe that the imputation made by him would harm the reputation of the complainant. The criminal offence emphasises on the intention or harm. Section 44 IPC defines "injury". It denotes any harm whatever illegally caused to any person, in body, mind, reputation or property. Thus, the word "injury" encapsulates harm caused to the reputation of any person. It also takes into account the harm caused to a person's body and mind. Section 499 provides for harm caused to the reputation of a person, that is, the complainant."(emphasis supplied) In the present case, the complainant has examined himself and his wife to prove harm to reputation. In this testimony, he has stated that the image of himself and his wife got tarnished after receipt of the letter in the department. Further, it is deposed, "We both felt defamed and thereafter I filed the present complaint." CW2 has also deposed on similar lines and has stated that due to the impugned letter, the reputation of her and her husband was seriously harmed. 24. However, in the considered opinion of this Court, the DEV Digitally signed by Ct. No. 4999490/2016 SUNIL PREM MASIH VS. H.N. POTTER Page 15 of 18 CHAUDHAR DEV CHAUDHARY Date: 2021.08.21 Y 14:16:48 +05'30' testimonies of the witnesses only point towards the fact that the witnesses felt that their reputation has been harmed. No specific utterances made by other people to the complainant has been deposed about. Further, no third person has been examined to prove that the publication of defamatory material caused the reputation of the complainant to be lowered in the estimation of other people like his friends, colleagues etc. CW2 has in her testimony said that her reputation was harmed, however, she is not a complainant in the present case. Further, a bald assertion that the reputation of her husband was also harmed, cannot be construed as cogent evidence regarding the loss of reputation of the complainant. In this regard, the observations of the Hon'ble Bombay High Court in Sudhir Parihar vs. Ravi Shankar Singh Bhadauria 2007 SCC OnLine Bom 1257 are pertinent. The Hon'ble Court held, inter alia, as under -: "13. The essence of the offence of defamation, is the harm caused to the reputation of a person. Character is what a person actually is and reputation is what neighbours and others say he is. In other words, reputation is a composite hearsay and which is the opinion of the community against a person. Everyone is entitled to have a very high estimate of himself but reputation is the estimation in which a person is held by others. The commission of offence of defamation or publishing any imputations concerning any person must be "intending to harm or knowing or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm, the reputation of such person. The complaint in this case was filed on 12.10.2004. The Complainant did not even chose to examine any of his friends who had, if at all read the contends of the said letter and found that on account of the same, the Complainant's reputation had suffered." Therefore, the complainant has failed to adduce any cogent evidence on record in support of his assertions regarding harm to his reputation. OTHER INCONSISTENCIES - 25.
