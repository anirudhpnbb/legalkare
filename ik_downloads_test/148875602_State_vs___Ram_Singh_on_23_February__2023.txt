It is settled that the initial burden is on the Prosecution to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt. So far as Sec. 7 of the POC Act is concerned, once it is proved by the Prosecution that the accused accepted any amount over & above the stamp paper value, Sec. 20 of the POC Act would raise a presumption against the accused. But it is well settled that before Sec. 20 of the POC Act can be brought in picture, the Prosecution must establish its foundational facts beyond reasonable doubt. Even as per Sec. 20 of the PC Act, only when it is proved that an accused accepted, obtained, or agreed to accept or, attempted to obtain any gratification other than legal remuneration, it shall be presumed that he accepted, obtained or agreed to accept or attempted to obtain that gratification as a motive or reward mentioned in Sec. 7 . Sub- Section 3 of Sec. 20 also provides that the Court may decline to draw presumption referred to in this Section if the gratification is so trivial that no inference of corruption may fairly be drawn. Even in a case under Prevention of Corruption Act , the onus is on the prosecution to prove the fundamental requirement of criminal law viz., the guilt of an accused should be proved beyond all reasonable doubts. 8.1. In State of Maharashtra Vs. Dnyaneshwar Laxman Rao Wankhede 2009 (4) RCR (criminal) 217, and State of Punjab Vs. Madan Mohan Lal Verma 2013 (7) LRC 34 (SC), it was held that the foundational facts must be established by the prosecution. It was also held that even while invoking the provision of section 20 of PC Act, the court is required to consider the explanation offered by the accused, if any, only on the touch stone of preponderance of probability and not on the touch stone of proof beyond all reasonable doubt. In the case of Madan Mohan (Supra) it was also observed that mere receipt of the amount by the accused is not sufficient to fasten guilt in absence of any evidence with regard to any demand and acceptance of Judgment dated 23-02-2023; CNR No. DLCT11-000618-2022 CC No. 60/2022 State Vs. Ram Singh FIR No.153/2012 P.S Daryaganj Page 9 of 25 10 the amount as illegal gratification. 8.2. It may also be mentioned here that it is well settled that it is one of the fundamental principles of criminal jurisprudence that an accused is presumed to be innocent till he is proved to be guilty. It is also well settled that suspicion however strong can never take the place of proof. There is a long distance between the fact that accused 'may have committed the offence' and 'must have committed the offence', which is to be proved by the prosecution by adducing reliable and cogent evidence. Presumption of innocence is recognised as human right which cannot be wished away. (Refer; Narender Singh & anr. Vs. State of M.P. (2004) 10 SCC 699; Ranjtsingh Brahamjeet Singh Sharma Vs. State of Maharashtra & Ors. (2005) 5 SCC 294; Ganesan Vs. Rama S. Raghuraman & Ors. (2011) 2 SCC 83 and; State of U.P. Vs. Naresh & Ors. (2011) 4 SCC 384.) 9. Assuming for the sake of arguments that the accused sold the stamp paper Ex.MO-2 to PW1 by overcharging Rs. 10/-, even then the allegations as set out would not have attracted Sec. 7 or Sec. 13(1)(d) of the POC Act, 1988. In order to attract Sec. 420 of IPC, it was necessary for the prosecution to have proved that the accused by deceiving PW1, fraudulently and dishonestly induced PW1 to deliver extra amount of Rs. 10/- to the accused. So far as Sec. 69 of the Indian Stamp Act, 1899 is concerned, it makes punishable where a licensed stamp vendor disobeys any rule made U/s 74 of the Act. There are two sub clauses under Sec. 69 . The first clause makes punishable violation of rules Judgment dated 23-02-2023; CNR No. DLCT11-000618-2022 CC No. 60/2022 State Vs. Ram Singh FIR No.153/2012 P.S Daryaganj Page 24 of 25 25 made under Sec. 74 . The second clause makes punishable an act of selling or offering for sale any stamp paper by a person not so appointed to sell. U/s 74 of the Act, the State Government is empowered to make rules. Delhi Province Stamp Rules, 1934 were framed U/s 74 of Indian Stamp Act . Rule 28(vii) of the said rules provides that a stamp vendor shall not demand or accept for any stamp any consideration exceeding the value of such stamp. Rule 29 also provides that infringement of any of the rules shall not only render cancellation of licence but also penalties U/s 69 of the Indian Stamp Act . Thus, had it been proved by the prosecution that the accused demanded or accepted any consideration exceeding the stamp value of Rs. 50/-, it would have violated Rule 28 of DPS Rules and rendered the accused liable for punishment U/s 69 of the Indian Stamp Act . However, as discussed above the Prosecution failed in proving the basic and foundational facts of demand or acceptance of money by the accused qua sale of stamp paper of Rs. 50/- and therefore there is no question of punishment U/s 69 of the Indian Stamp Act . 17.
