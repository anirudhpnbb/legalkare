By way of this petition brought under Section 397 CrPC, the revisionist has assailed order dated 18.02.2020 of the learned magistrate whereby application under Section 156(3) CrPC of the revisionist complainant in the complaint case for offences under Section 354A / 499 / 500 / 506 / 120B / 34 IPC was dismissed, directing the revisionist complainant to lead pre­summoning evidence. Upon service of notice, the proposed accused persons arrayed herein as respondents no. 2­4 entered appearance through counsel. I heard learned counsel for both sides as well as learned prosecutor on behalf of State. There was no necessity to summon the original trial court record since certified copies of relevant record were filed by revisionist. 2. Briefly stated, the circumstances leading to the present revision petition are as follows. 2.1 On 16.03.2019, a complaint was lodged by the revisionist at PS Burari, which was recorded as DD No. 67B. In the said complaint, the revisionist stated that she had taken loan from Kangra Co­operative Cr. Revision No. 225/2020 Page 2 of 8 pages Smt. Ruby Kumari vs. State & Ors . Bank but due to financial constraints, she could not pay back certain installments, so the bank had filed certain litigation; that on 16.03.2019 in the evening, three officials of the bank visited her residence and asked her to accompany them in their car to the bank, for which she refused; that those bank officials kept talking to her discourteously and she called her husband after which they went away; that her husband called PCR; that one of those bank officials was Arun Awasthi and when her husband called up on mobile phone of Arun Awasthi, the latter denied having visited her home, though the entire incident is recorded in CCTV camera, so appropriate action be taken. 2.2 Thereafter on 25.04.2019, another complaint was lodged by the revisionist complainant at PS Burari, which was recorded as DD No. 68B. In the said second complaint, the revisionist complainant alleged that on 16.03.2019, three officials from the Kangra Co­operative Bank visited her residence at about 04:20 pm and one of them called her out and asked her name; that handing over a paper to her, that person uttered the words : "Bahanchod tum Biharan Chor ho, ek saal se main court ke chakkar kaat raha hu. Main tujhe rupaya court se lene ke liye nahi diya tha. Ye paper order letter hai. Aap mere sath chalo"; that she called her husband who immediately reached and intervened, so those officials abused him, calling him Bihari; that her husband called PCR but for two hours no PCR came, so she lodged her complaint at the police station at 07:30 pm, but the police officials did not allow her to write correct statement and took no action. The learned magistrate called for Status Report from local police in which identity of those two unknown persons was revealed and they have been impleaded as respondents no. 3 and 4 in these proceedings. 2.4 In its detailed Status Report dated 23.01.2020, the local police concluded that the allegations leveled by the revisionist complainant were found to be not true as apparently, the present private respondents had visited the residence of the complainant in the course of a loan recovery drive while meeting all defaulters and had requested the complainant revisionist and her husband to clear the outstanding amount; that when they served recovery notice, the complainant and her husband destroyed the same and abused and threatened them; and that the bank had already initiated proceedings under Section 138 Negotiable Instruments Act as well as Arbitration proceedings. 2.5 After examining the above material on record, the learned magistrate dismissed the application under Section 156(3) CrPC, observing that identity of the proposed accused persons has been disclosed and in the overall circumstances he did not feel inclined to invoke discretionary powers under Section 156(3) CrPC. Cr. Revision No. 225/2020 Page 5 of 8 pages Smt. Ruby Kumari vs. State & Ors . 6. At this stage, it would be apposite to briefly traverse through the legal position relevant for present purposes. 7. In the case of Arvindbhai Rajivbhai Patel vs Dhirubhai Sabhubhai, 1998 (1) Crimes 351, the Hon'ble High Court took a strong exception to the growing tendency of asking the police to investigate the cases under Section 156 (3) CrPC. The Hon'ble High Court observed that some scheming, manipulating complainants are interested in securing order under Section 156(3) CrPC with a view to strike unjust, illegal and unfair bargain with accused with the help of police pressure. 8. In the case of Rashmi Malhotra vs SHO , 98(2002) DLT 283, the Hon'ble Delhi High Court held that discretion vests in the Magistrate to either take cognizance without any inquiry into the complaint or to direct investigation under Section 156(3) CrPC or to postpone the same after preliminary inquiry and the complainant cannot insist for following any particular mode for taking cognizance. 9. In the case of Skipper Beverages vs State , 2001 (3) RCR (Criminal) 514, where the allegation of the complainant was regarding theft, the Hon'ble Delhi High Court upheld the view that such allegations could be proved by way of oral or other evidence, without seeking police intervention. In the said case, even as regards forgery allegation, the Hon'ble Delhi High Court took a view that the same also did not require any police intervention. Cr. Revision No. 225/2020 Page 6 of 8 pages Smt. Ruby Kumari vs. State & Ors . 10. In the case of Subhkaran Luharuka vs State , 170 (2010) DLT 516, the Hon'ble Delhi High Court quashed the FIR for offence under Section 420/467/468/471/ 120 ­B/34 IPC , which had been registered by police on the basis of complaint under Section 156(3) CrPC. Traversing through about 40 judicial precedents cited by both sides, the Hon'ble Delhi High Court examined various provisions of Chapters XII and XV of the Criminal Procedure Code and held thus : "52A .... (iv) ofcourse it is open to the Magistrate to proceed under Chapter XII of the Code when an application under Section 156 (3) of the Code is also filed alongwith a complaint under Section 200 of the Code. If the Magistrate decides not to take cognizance of the complaint, however , in that case , the Magistrate before passing any order to proceed under Chapter XII should not only satisfy himself about the pre­requisites as aforesaid, but additionally, he should also be satisfied that it is necessary to direct police investigation in the matter for collection of evidence which is neither in the possession of the complaint nor can be produced by the witnesses on being summoned by the court at the instance of complainant, and the matter is such which calls for investigation by a State agency. The Magistrate must pass an order giving cogent reasons as to why he intends to proceed under Chapter XII instead of Chapter XV of the Code." (emphasis supplied) 11. Falling back to the present case, as mentioned above, in the very first complaint dated 16.03.2019 recorded as DD No. 67B by PS Burari, no cognizable offence was made out and it is only subsequently Cr. Revision No. 225/2020 Page 7 of 8 pages Smt. Ruby Kumari vs. State & Ors . vide complaint dated 25.04.2019 recorded as DD No. 68B by PS Burari that certain cognizable offences came out. At this stage and in these proceedings it would not be appropriate to further analyse on the said two complaints. Suffice it to record that genesis of the alleged incident needs to be examined meticulously by the trial court. 12. Further, as rightly observed by the learned magistrate in the impugned order, identity of the proposed accused persons has already been disclosed in the Status Report dated 18.02.2020 of the local police and in that regard, no further investigation by police is required. There is no evidence for which police investigation is required in this case since identity of the alleged assailants and all relevant facts are already within the knowledge of the revisionist and she cannot insist on the mode of cognizance. 13. I am unable to find any illegality, incorrectness or impropriety in the impugned order, so the same is upheld. The revision petition is devoid of merits, so the same is dismissed. 14.
