It is a paramount tenet of criminal law that every accused is presumed to be innocent and cannot be convicted unless the prosecution is able to discharge the initial onus rested upon it beyond all reasonable doubts. The failure to do so would necessarily result in acquittal of accused. It has been held by Hon'ble Punjab & Haryana High Court in Sadhu Singh Vs. State of Punjab (1997) 3 RCR (Cri) 421:­ "5. In a criminal trial, it is for the prosecution to establish its case beyond all reasonable doubts. It is for the prosecution to travel the entire distance from 'may have' to 'must have'. If the prosecution appears to be improbable or lacks credibility the benefit of doubt necessarily has to go to the accused." 12. At the outset, it is alleged that complaint was criminally defamed by accused no.1. The provision of Section 500 IPC prescribes punishment for defamation which is defined under Section 499 IPC. The offence of defamation consists of following three essential ingredients, all of which must be established by complainant against accused no.1 to drive home her guilt:­ Mange Ram Vs. Sangeeta & Ors. CNR no. DLSW020004602016 Page no.6/13 Digitally signed by BHARTI BHARTI GARG GARG Date: 2022.10.15 16:27:34 +0530 a) making or publishing any imputation concerning any person, b) such imputation must have been made by words either spoken or by visible representations, and c) such imputation must be made with the intention, to cause harm or with the knowledge or having reasons to believe that it will harm reputation of the person concerned. 13. Therefore, it is amply clear that the intention or knowledge to cause harm is a pre­requisite to attract penal liability for the act of defamation. Further, Section 499 IPC is subject to ten exceptions laid down therein. Accused no.1 has primarily relied on Exception 8 in her defence, which stipulates that 'it is not defamation to prefer in good faith an accusation against any person to any of those who have lawful authority over that person with respect to the subject­matter of accusation.' 14. Elucidating the law in regard to eighth exception to Section 499 IPC, it has been held by Hon'ble Supreme Court in Rajendra Kumar Sitaram Pande and others Vs. Uttam and another (1999) 3 SCC 134 that:­ "The question for consideration is whether the allegations in the complaint read with the report of the Magistrate make out the offence under Section 500 or not Section 499 of the IPC defines the offence of defamation and Section 500 provides the punishment for such offence. Exception 8 to Section 499 clearly indicates that it is not a defamation to prefer in good faith an accusation against any person to any of those who have lawful authority over that person with regard to the subject matter of accusation. The report of the Treasury Officer clearly indicates that Mange Ram Vs. Sangeeta & Ors. CNR no. DLSW020004602016 Page no.7/13 Digitally signed by BHARTI BHARTI GARG Date: GARG 2022.10.15 16:27:45 pursuant to the report made by the accused persons against the complaint, a departmental inquiry had been initiated that the complainant was found to be guilty. Under such circumstances the fact that the accused persons had made a report to the superior officer of the complainant alleging that he had abused to the Treasury Officer in a drunken state which is the gravamen of the present complaint ad nothing more, would be covered by exception 8 to Section 499 of IPC. By perusing the allegations made in the complaint petition, we are also satisfied that no case of defamation has been made out." 15. Needless to mention, the burden of proving said exception lies on accused no.1 according to Section 105 of The Indian Evidence Act, which ought to be discharged by her on preponderance of probabilities. It is also well settled that such exceptions may be proved by her on the basis of material available on record. In fact, there may be cases where though the exception is not proved, evidence on record gives rise to doubt regarding an element, such as intention, which is an ingredient of the offence, in which case the accused shall have to be acquitted. Guidance in that regard is sought from the judgment of Hon'ble High Court of Allahabad in Rishi Kesh Singh and Others Vs. State AIR 1970 All 51. 16. It needs to be iterated that for taking the benefit of eighth exception under Section 499 IPC, the accused no.1 is not required to prove that the aspersions cast by her against the complainant were true. That incidentally lies within the ambit of first exception. The eighth exception has been designed to meet cases where a person bona fide believes that another person has caused harm to him/her and complains about the same to one who has a bounden duty to enquire into such complaint. At this juncture, reliance is placed upon the following excerpt from the judgment of Hon'ble Madras High Court in N.Sathya and another Vs. V.Sekar (2009) 3 MWN (Cri) 266:­ "18. To obtain the protection given by this Exception 8 to Section 499 of IPC, (1) the accusation must be made to a person in authority over the party accused and (2) the accusation must be preferred in good faith. So, it is clear that if without express malice, a person makes a defamation charge, which he bona fide believes to be true, against one whose conduct has caused him injury, to one whose duty is to enquire into and redress such injury, the occasion is privileged, because the person making the charge has an interest in its subject matter and the person to whom the Mange Ram Vs. Sangeeta & Ors. CNR no. DLSW020004602016 Page no.11/13 Digitally signed by BHARTI BHARTI GARG GARG Date: 2022.10.15 16:28:28 +0530 communication is made has, on hearing it, a duty to discharge in respect of it. 19. In order to come within the Exception 8 toSection 499 of IPC , the Petitioners are not bound to prove that the allegations made by them are true. It is sufficient if they prove that on reasonable grounds he believed the allegations to be true and in that belief they bona fide made the accusation to the lawful authority." 23. In light of the foregoing discussion, this court opines on the premise of material brought on record by the complainant that the act of accused no.1 resorting to lawful authorities to raise the grievance of injury allegedly caused by the complainant on her person is squarely excepted by law. The intention of accused no.1 was rather to protect her interest and seek relief from the authorities. The complainant has failed to show that such an accusation was made by accused no.1 with the intention to degrade his reputation, and not with the intention to redress her grievance. 24. Moving further, the admission of complainant that case in FIR no.82/2011 PS J.P.Kalan lodged by him against accused no.1 regarding the incident which occurred on 08.09.2011 was also dismissed by the court goes on to weaken the case of complainant in the present case. In addition to this, the complainant has not adduced any evidence nor examined any other person to prove that his estimation in the eyes of others was lowered by such accusation. Therefore, it is not established with certitude that his reputation was harmed before society or third person. Mange Ram Vs. Sangeeta & Ors. CNR no. To recapitulate the aforesaid discussion, the lodging of criminal case by accused no.1 against the complainant in FIR no.83/2011 is protected by eighth exception under Section 499 IPC and hence, the complainant has failed to prove that accused no.1 committed the offence of defamation with the intention to harm the reputation of complainant. The complainant has also not produced any evidence to prove that his reputation was in fact, lowered before third person. As a sequitur thereto, accused no.1 stands entitled to benefit of doubt. Resultantly, since the complainant has failed in proving its case beyond reasonable doubts against accused no.1, accused no.1 Sangeeta D/o Sh.
