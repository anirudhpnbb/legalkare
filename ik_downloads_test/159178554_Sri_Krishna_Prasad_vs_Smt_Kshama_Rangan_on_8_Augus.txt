I have considered the submissions made by the learned counsel for the petitioners/accused as well as the respondents/complainants. 15. Any pleading filed before any Court of law is bound to contain what the parties believe to be true and proceedings in the case would begin on that assumption. Therefore, when a party makes a pleading in a Court of law, he is bound to prove every bit of it. In the case on hand, a dispute essentially between the husband and the wife has taken into its fold the members of the extended family of the parties. In a proceeding for appointment of a guardian filed by the wife, the husband made several caustic allegations not only against his 21 wife but also against her brother-in-law, her brother-in- law's father and others. A prima-facie look at the allegations made do indicate that if they were not proved, it not only defames the wife but also outrage her modesty. It also affected the relationship of the wife (Smt. Kshama) and her brother-in-law and her brother- in-law's father. These allegations though made in the objections filed by the husband, they were verified to be true and correct by the family members of the husband who are also the petitioners in these cases. Therefore, it has to be construed that all the petitioners stood by the allegations made against the wife (Smt. Kshama) and the members of her extended family. The claim of the husband and his relatives that the proceeding for defamation would not lie until the conclusion of the proceeding in G & WC No.85/2016 is liable to be rejected as the allegations do give rise to cause of action to prosecute the petitioners for an action of defamation. It is also relevant to note that an action for defamation 22 has to be brought within a prescribed period and therefore, it cannot wait for the outcome of proceeding in G & WC No.85/2016. Even otherwise, Sri. Shreyas and his father are not parties in G & WC No.85/2016 and thus, the question of the Court giving any finding concerning them is ruled out. 16. The contention of the petitioners that the accusation made in G & WC No.85/2016 falls within the Ninth Exception to Section 499 of IPC, cannot be taken note of in a proceeding under Section 482 of Cr.P.C., as the exception to Section 499 of IPC is a defence in an action for defamation. The question whether the petitioners made these allegations in good faith for protection of their interest, is a matter for trial before the Magistrate and this Court cannot certainly entertain this contention in a proceeding under Section 482 of Cr.P.C. 23 17. Coming to the most formidable contention of the petitioners that publication of the defamatory article is sine qua non for an action for defamation, Explanation 4 to Section 499 of IPC holds the answer where it says, "No imputation is said to harm a person's reputation, unless that imputation directly or indirectly, in the estimation of others, lowers the moral or intellectual character of that person, or lowers the character of that person in respect of his caste or of his calling, or lowers the credit of that person, or causes it to be believed that the body of that person is in a loathsome state or in a state generally considered as disgraceful." 18. The petitioners cannot claim that the pleadings made in Court proceedings are not capable of being published by word of mouth or otherwise. The pleadings are accessible not only to the friends and relatives of the parties but also to the Advocates conducting the cases, their colleagues etc., Therefore, 24 the contention of the petitioners that publication is a sine qua non for an action for defamation, is liable to be rejected. Even otherwise, the question whether it is published or the extent of publication are all matters of trial before the Magistrate and this Court cannot usurp the jurisdiction of the Magistrate by quashing the proceedings on a question of fact. 19. In that view of the matter, all these petitions lack merits and are dismissed. The learned Magistrate is directed to conclude the proceedings at the earliest. Sd/- JUDGE SRT/PMR
