I have heard counsel for the parties and perused the records. The vital question to be decided in the present proceedings is 'whether the respondents can maintain criminal prosecution for offence under section 500 IPC beyond a period of three years from the date of lodging of FIR by complainant Sarwan Singh in which the petitioners appeared as witnesses for the prosecution?'. The issue is no longer res intgra in view of the judgment passed by Hon'ble the Supreme Court in Surinder Mohan Vikal 's case (supra), relied upon by the petitioners. In the said case, the appellant filed a complaint against the respondent for commission of offence under Sections 406 and 420 IPC on 15.03.1972. Vide judgment dated 11.02.1975, the Magistrate convicted the respondent and one Amreek Singh for offence under Sections 408 / 34 IPC and sentenced them accordingly. The Additional Sessions Judge acquitted both of them by judgment dated 01.04.1975 and that judgment was upheld by the High Court on 15.05.1975. Respondent Ascharaj Lal Chopra filed a complaint against appellant Surinder Mohan Vikal in the Court of Judicial Magistrate First Class, Ambala dated 11.02.1976 for commission of offence under Section 500 IPC. The Magistrate examined the complainant and the witnesses and made order dated 15.09.1976 for issuance of summons for appearance of the present appellant in that case . The appellant filed a petition before the High Court under Section 482 of the Code for quashing the Magistrate's order Bimbra Mohan Lal 2014.04.01 16:14 I attest to the accuracy and integrity of this document Chandigarh CRM M 45161 of 2007 and connected matter 5 taking cognizance of the offence against him. As his petition was rejected by the High Court, accused Surinder Mohan Vikal preferred appeal before the Hon'ble Apex Court. The Hon'ble Court in para 4 of the judgment raised the question to be answered in the following terms:- "The question is when the period of limitation could be said to commence for purposes of the present case." The Court in para 8 of the judgment held quoted hereinbelow:- "8. It would thus appear that the appellant was entitled to the benefit of sub Section (1) of S. 468 which prohibits every Court from taking cognizance of an offence of the category specified in sub section (2) after expiry of the period of limitation. It is hardly necessary to say that statutes of limitation have legislative policy behind them. For instance, they shut out belated and dormant claims in order to save the accused from unnecessary harassment. They also save the accused from the risk of having to face trial at a time when his evidence might have been lost because of the delay on the part of the prosecutor. As has been stated, a bar to the taking of cognizance has been prescribed under S. 468 of the Cr.P.C and there is no reason why the appellant should not be entitled to it in the facts and circumstances of this case." Accordingly, appeal was allowed, judgment passed by this Court was set aside and order of the Magistrate dated 15.09.1976 taking cognizance of the offence against the appellant was quashed. Keeping in view the authoritative enunciation of law laid down by Hon'ble the Supreme Court in Surinder Mohan Vikal 's case (supra), I am Bimbra Mohan Lal 2014.04.01 16:14 I attest to the accuracy and integrity of this document Chandigarh CRM M 45161 of 2007 and connected matter 6 unable to accept the submissions of counsel for the respondents that the petitioners are not entitled to seek relief in the present proceedings or the matter is required to be left to be decided by the trial Court. The judgment in Surinder Mohan Vikal 's case (supra) squarely covers the controversy raised in the present case. The petitioners, therefore, are entitled to the relief claimed as the summoning order passed by the Judicial Magistrate taking cognizance of the offence cannot be allowed to sustain. It is pertinent to mention here that counsel for the respondents, despite being provided an opportunity, could not cite any contrary law and fairly conceded that the judgment in Surinder Mohan Vikal 's case (supra) is in favour of the petitioners. Apart from the legal issue raised by the petitioners and answered in their favour, I find force in the contention of the petitioners that the complainant or the prosecution witnesses cannot be subject to criminal proceedings under Section 500 IPC merely because the accused in the criminal proceedings have not been found guilty or acquitted of the offence as has been so held by this Court in the judgments cited by counsel for the petitioners. In view of what has been discussed hereinabove, CRM M 45161 of 2007 is allowed, order dated 30.04.2003 (Annexure P3) and proceedings subsequent thereto are ordered to be quashed. As a natural corollary, CRR No. 2798 of 2009 is ordered to be dismissed, leaving the parties to bear their own costs.
