Facts and circumstances giving rise to file present application are that - (1) The private complaint for the offences as referred above is filed by respondent complainant against 83 persons, inter-alia alleging that, the parties are belong to Waghela community and the applicant - accused and others named in the compliant are representing the rival group. It is alleged in the complaint that on 25.07.2014, the meeting was convened by the accused, with respect to Trust namely Waghela Jagirdar Samaj Vikas Trust. It is alleged that, in the meeting, it was resolved that, the complainant, without taking sense of community have illegally and arbitrarily created the Trust. It is further alleged that said resolution was circulated amongst the community and also made allegation against the complainant and others that trust was created to divide the Waghela community and have also made defamatory statement to harm the reputation of the complainant and others. (2) In the aforesaid facts, on 20.01.2015, the private complaint is being filed against 83 persons, before the Magisterial Court at Palanpur. Having heard the learned counsel for the respective parties and upon perusal of the allegations leveled in the complaint, the issue falls for my consideration is whether the applicants have made out a case for quashing of the complaint ? 11. The scope and ambit of the power by the High Court under Section 482 Cr.P.C., to quash the FIR and/or private complaint has been expounded by the Apex Court in a catana of decisions. In the celebrated decision in case of State of Haryana Vs. Bhajanlal (1992) Suppl.1 P- 335, the Apex Court considered in detail the scope of the High Court's power under Section 482 to quash the criminal proceedings and referred to several judicial precedents and laid down the following parameters in which, the FIR/complaint can be quashed: "102. In the backdrop of the interpretation of the various relevant provisions of the Code under Chapter XIV and of the principles of law enunciated by this Court in a series of decisions relating to the exercise of the extraordinary power under Article 226 or the inherent powers under Section 482 of the Page 6 of 11 Downloaded on : Sun Sep 17 18:35:32 IST 2023 NEUTRAL CITATION R/SCR.A/3288/2015 ORDER DATED: 17/04/2023 undefined Code which we have extracted and reproduced above, we give the following categories of cases by way of illustration wherein such power could be exercised either to prevent abuse of the process of any court or otherwise to secure the ends of justice, though it may not be possible to lay down any precise, clearly defined and sufficiently channelised and inflexible guidelines or rigid formulae and to give an exhaustive list of myriad kinds of cases wherein such power should be exercised: (1) Where the allegations made in the first information report or the complaint, even if they are taken at their face value and accepted in their entirety do not prima facie constitute any offence or make out a case against the accused. (2) Where the allegations in the first information report and other materials, if any, accompanying the FIR do not disclose a cognizable offence, justifying an investigation by police officers under Section 156(1) of the Code except under an order of a Magistrate within the purview of Section 155(2) of the Code. (3) Where the uncontroverted allegations made in the FIR or complaint and the evidence collected in support of the same do not disclose the commission of any offence and make out a case against the accused. (4) Where, the allegations in the FIR do not constitute a cognizable offence but constitute only a non-cognizable offence, no investigation is permitted by a police officer without an order of a Magistrate as contemplated under Section 155(2) of the Code. (5) Where the allegations made in the FIR or complaint are so absurd and inherently improbable on the basis of which no prudent person can ever reach a just conclusion that there is sufficient ground for proceeding against the accused. Page 7 of 11 Downloaded on : Sun Sep 17 18:35:32 IST 2023 NEUTRAL CITATION R/SCR.A/3288/2015 ORDER DATED: 17/04/2023 undefined (6) Where there is an express legal bar engrafted in any of the provisions of the Code or the Act concerned (under which a criminal proceeding is instituted) to the institution and continuance of the proceedings and/or where there is a specific provision in the Code or the Act concerned, providing efficacious redress for the grievance of the aggrieved party. (7) Where a criminal proceeding is manifestly attended with mala fides and/or where the proceeding is maliciously instituted with an ulterior motive for wreaking vengeance on the accused and with a view to spite him due to private and personal grudge." 12. Having regard to the facts and circumstances to the present case and on careful reading of the compliant, this Court is of the opinion that, the complainant and accused belong to same community and caste. The dispute arose between the two group of the community, when the complainant and other persons of their group created a community Trust. The accused and others were of the opinion that the Trust was created to divide the community. It is in this context, the case is required to be appreciated. In order to establish the defamatory contents of the alleged resolution, allegedly passed by the accused party, the complainant failed to produce it before the trial Court. The police while making inquiry, did not find any such resolution, containing therein any imputation allegedly made by the accused to harm the reputation of the complainant and others. The accused have been charged under Section 499 of the Indian Penal Code. The Page 8 of 11 Downloaded on : Sun Sep 17 18:35:32 IST 2023 NEUTRAL CITATION R/SCR.A/3288/2015 ORDER DATED: 17/04/2023 undefined persons who publishes as well as the person who made the defamatory imputations are fall under the ambit of 'defamation' as defined under Section 499 of the Indian Penal Code. The offence of defamation consists of three essential ingredients, namely (i) making or publishing any imputation concerning any person, (ii) such imputations must have been made by words, either spoken or intended to be read or by sign or by visible representation, (iii) the said imputation must have been made with intention to harm or with knowledge or having reasons to believe that it will harm the reputation of the person concerned. 13. In light of the statutory provisions and the allegations made in the complaint, prima-facie, this Court is of the considered opinion that the complainant is failed to establish the fact of the resolution and publication thereof amongst the members of the community. Except the bald statement, nothing incriminating material is placed on record to establish that the accused have made defamatory statement to harm the reputation of the complainant and others. Thus, therefore, the allegations made in the complaint do not constitute any offence or make out a case under Section 499 , 500 , 501 and 506(2) of the IPC against the accused. The learned trial Court while taking cognizance of the offence, did not have assigned brief reasons on the aspect of sufficient grounds to proceed against the accused. It is settled law that, the order should not contain a detailed reasons but atleast a brief reasons are required to be assigned by the Court to Page 9 of 11 Downloaded on : Sun Sep 17 18:35:32 IST 2023 NEUTRAL CITATION R/SCR.A/3288/2015 ORDER DATED: 17/04/2023 undefined arrive at a conclusion that there are sufficient grounds for proceedings. Reference can be made on the decision in case of Pepsi Foods Limited Vs. Special Judicial Magistrate , (1998) 5 SCC 749, wherein, the Apex Court observed that, summoning of an accused in a criminal case is a serious matter. Criminal law cannot set into motion as a matter of course. In para-28, it was observed and held that, the order of the Magistrate summoning the accused must reflects that he has applied his mind to the case and law applicable thereto. He has to examine the nature of allegations made in the complaint and evidence in support thereof and then, examine if any offence is prima-facie committed by the accused and record his satisfaction about the prima-facie case. 14. For the foregoing reasons, this Court is of the opinion that, due to inter-se dispute of community, the proceedings of the criminal case has been initiated without their being any evidence to establish the essential ingredients of the offences as referred above . The learned trial Court while taking cognizance of the offence, did not have properly examined the nature of allegations made in the complaint and law applicable thereto. On the perusal of the impugned order, summoning the accused, it appears that, the court miserably failed to record his satisfaction about the prima-facie case against the accused and in casual manner, issued a process. In such circumstances, the accusations made in the criminal case, if taken at their face value and accepted in their entirety, do not constitute an offence under Sections 499 , 500 , 501 and 506(2) of the IPC. The case is fully covered by categories 1 and 7, as enumerated by the Apex Court in case of Bhajanlal (supra). 16. In the aforesaid discussions, this Court is convinced that, to prevent the abuse of process of court and for the ends of justice, this is a fit case to exercise inherent powers under Section 482 to quash the criminal case being CC No. 515 of 2015 and consequential proceedings therefrom against qua present applicants. 17. In the result, the petition is allowed to aforesaid extent. Rule is made absolute. Direct service permitted. (ILESH J. VORA,J) P.S.
